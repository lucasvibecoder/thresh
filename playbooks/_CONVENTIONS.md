# Playbook Conventions

Rules that apply at the **playbook-artifact level** — copy register, structure, slot patterns. These are graduated rules from `playbooks/feedback-log.md` (cross-validated claims that left the registry) plus any playbook-specific patterns established directly.

Cold-email-level principles live in memory files (`~/.claude/projects/.../memory/feedback_outbound_*.md`), not here. This file is for the artifact you ship — the playbook web page, the cover materials, the framing — not the individual email mechanics.

## When to read this

- Before building a new playbook
- Before revising an existing playbook's copy or structure
- When a claim graduates from `feedback-log.md` and lands here

## Playbook copy register

**Avoid jargon density and AI-detector patterns at the artifact level.**

- **Status:** cross-validated 2026-05-08
- **Sources:** Le (interim Head of Marketing / advisor at Assured — Seed health-tech, marketing-leader vantage) + Sage (AE at HiBob — Series D HRIS, sales-operator vantage). Two independent voices, different industries, different roles, same week.
- **Why:** Sophisticated recipients pattern-match against AI-generated copy and bail on jargon density. Le's word: "convoluted." Sage's word: "sounds like AI." This applies to the **playbook artifact's prose** — section headers, framing copy, role-play diagnostics — not just the email cards within it.
- **How to apply:**
  - Read the playbook's prose out loud. If a sentence requires three jargon-laden clauses to land, rewrite it.
  - Specific jargon to watch: "leverage," "operational substrate," "credentialing throughput math," abstract nouns stacked together (e.g., "data-source curation methodology activation"). When the artifact reads like a McKinsey deck, sophisticated buyers downgrade trust.
  - Concrete > abstract. "90 days to credential a provider" beats "credentialing-throughput optimization."
  - Short clauses > long clauses. If a sentence has more than two commas, it probably needs splitting.
  - The role-play and "what got cut" sections are where jargon hides — they're internal-feeling, so the writer often relaxes there. Don't.
- **Counter-conditions:** Doesn't apply when the artifact's audience is explicitly technical (e.g., a deeply-detailed methodology white paper for an engineering buyer). Most Thresh playbooks are not in that category — buyers are GTM operators, ops leaders, or marketing leaders who get pitched dozens of these per month.
