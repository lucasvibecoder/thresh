## What Makes Thresh Fundamentally Different

### 1. The data is different.

Every other outbound agency, Clay shop, and "signal-based" service uses the same signals: job postings, funding rounds, tech stack changes, LinkedIn activity, website visits, G2 research. These are first-order signals — everyone has them, everyone uses them, and prospects get 30 emails a week triggered by the same data.

Thresh uses third-order signals that nobody else touches: FMCSA safety violations, OSHA citations, LL97 building compliance scores, EPA enforcement actions, DOT audit results, ENERGY STAR ratings, permit expirations, FDA warning letters. This data is public, but it's buried in government databases that sales teams don't know exist and wouldn't know how to use if they did.

The difference isn't incremental. It's categorical. A "signal" that says *"this company posted a VP of Engineering role"* tells you they might be growing. A signal that says *"this carrier has 4 out of 7 BASICs flagged and is one failed audit away from being shut down"* tells you they have a problem that costs money every day they don't fix it.

### 2. The messages are different.

When your data is different, your messages are different by default. You don't have to manufacture relevance with "I noticed your company is growing" icebreakers. You don't have to hope the prospect connects the dots between their situation and your product. The message IS the data.

*"277 Park Avenue scored 14 on ENERGY STAR — bottom 14% of comparable US office buildings. Under LL97, estimated annual penalty: $1.54M, nearly doubling when 2030 limits take effect."*

That's not personalization. That's intelligence. The prospect can verify every number. They can't ignore it because it's about THEIR building, THEIR penalty, THEIR deadline. No template, no icebreaker, no "saw your post" — just undeniable, specific, verifiable facts about their business.

### 3. The proof is different.

Intent data is probabilistic. A website visit *might* mean interest. A job posting *might* mean budget. A funding round *might* mean they're buying. You're guessing and hoping the timing is right.

Public compliance data is deterministic. A building either scored 14 on ENERGY STAR or it didn't. A carrier either has conditional status or it doesn't. A facility either failed its fire inspection or it didn't. There's no interpretation, no inference, no "based on our proprietary algorithm." The prospect can check the source themselves.

This changes the entire dynamic of the conversation. You're not selling — you're informing. You're not interrupting — you're delivering value. The message would be worth reading even if the prospect never buys anything.

### 4. The market is empty.

After researching 30+ companies in the "signal-based outbound" space (March 2026), the findings are clear:

- **One direct competitor** (Slingshot GTM) — early stage, low search visibility, $8K/mo minimum
- **Zero other agencies** using public/regulatory data as the core signal for outbound
- **20+ Clay agencies** competing on the same tooling at similar price points
- **Multiple "signal-based" agencies** that all use the same intent data (job changes, funding, website visits)
- **Several vertical data providers** (CarrierOK, ProsperFleet) that sell raw data but don't build campaigns

The gap between "here's a database of carriers with violations" and "here's a campaign targeting those carriers with messaging built around their specific compliance problems" is Thresh's entire positioning. Nobody occupies it.

## The Competitive Moat

Most outbound services are differentiated by *execution quality* — better copywriting, better deliverability, better automation. These are real advantages, but they're thin. Any good Clay agency can match them in months.

Thresh is differentiated by *data access + domain knowledge + signal interpretation*. To replicate what Thresh does, a competitor would need to:

1. **Know which government databases exist** for a given industry (FMCSA, OSHA, EPA, DOB, ENERGY STAR, FDA, SEC — and the hundreds of state-level equivalents)
2. **Know how to pull and structure that data** (these aren't APIs with nice docs — they're FOIA portals, PDF dumps, and legacy search interfaces)
3. **Know how to score and interpret the data** (what makes a violation severe vs. routine? What penalty thresholds matter? When does a signal decay?)
4. **Know how to translate data into messaging** that sounds like intelligence, not spam (the diagnostic chain: signal → data cocktail → tension → existential test → message type → output format → write)
5. **Have the outbound infrastructure** to actually deliver the messages (email verification, deliverability, CRM integration, contact enrichment)

Each step is learnable individually. The combination — especially steps 1-4 — is rare. And the diagnostic chain (the intellectual framework for reading tension and choosing message type) is proprietary knowledge that took months to develop.
